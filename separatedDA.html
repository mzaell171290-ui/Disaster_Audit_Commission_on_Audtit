DRRM
Assessment of Disaster Risk Reduction Management at the Local Level

A limited scoping and analysis of existing Disaster Risk Reduction and Management (DRRM) practices at the Local Government Unit level, drawing on existing literature, agency reports, and field validation. The assessment looks at how provincial, city, and municipal DRRM offices plan for, budget, and account for calamity funds, and identifies recurring gaps between what is allocated on paper and what actually reaches affected communities.

Key findings cover fund utilization rates, the timeliness of disbursements after a calamity declaration, and common documentation issues auditors encounter when tracing relief goods and cash aid back to their source appropriations.

TYPHOON YOLANDA
A Special Report on Typhoon Yolanda

This special report examines the disbursement and use of national and international relief funds following Typhoon Yolanda (Haiyan), one of the strongest storms on record to make landfall. It traces the flow of assistance from national agencies through local government units and down to the barangay level.

The report highlights lessons learned around emergency procurement rules, donor-fund reconciliation, and the rebuilding of damaged public infrastructure, with recommendations aimed at tightening controls for future large-scale disaster response.

CATEGORY
Citizen Participatory Audit

The Citizen Participatory Audit (CPA) program invites ordinary citizens, civil society groups, and community volunteers to take part directly in the audit process for disaster-related projects, from small flood-control structures to relief-goods warehousing.

By pairing trained COA auditors with community observers, the program builds local capacity to spot red flags early, such as ghost deliveries or overpriced materials, and strengthens public trust in how calamity funds are eventually accounted for.

FLOOD CONTROL
Audit of Flood Control Infrastructure Projects

A review of flood-control structures funded under national disaster mitigation programs, covering drainage systems, floodwalls, and pumping stations across flood-prone provinces. The audit checks whether completed structures match approved engineering plans and released funds.

Findings point to delayed completion timelines in several provinces and recommend stronger coordination between the Department of Public Works and Highways and local disaster councils.

RELIEF GOODS
Tracking Relief Goods Distribution

This report follows the movement of relief goods, food packs, and hygiene kits from national warehouses down to barangay distribution points after major typhoons. It cross-checks delivery receipts against beneficiary lists to spot gaps or duplication.

The audit recommends a standardized digital tracking system to replace manual logbooks currently used by most local government units.

EARLY WARNING
Evaluation of Early Warning Systems

An assessment of government-funded early warning equipment, including rain gauges, water level sensors, and community alert sirens installed in high-risk municipalities. The report examines maintenance records and response times during recent weather events.

It highlights several units left unmaintained since installation and recommends a regular inspection schedule tied to local disaster risk reduction budgets.

HISTORY
Evolution of Disaster Laws in the Philippines

The Philippine disaster management system traces its origin back to 1941, when President Manuel L. Quezon signed Executive Order No. 335, establishing the earliest framework for civilian emergency preparedness in the country. Over the following decades, this early structure was expanded and reorganized several times in response to major calamities, gradually shifting from a purely reactive, relief-oriented approach toward a more preventive, risk-reduction mindset.

That evolution culminated in the passage of the Philippine Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Act, which redefined how national and local agencies plan, budget, and coordinate before, during, and after a disaster. Understanding this legal history helps explain why today's audits look not only at how relief funds are spent, but also at how well agencies planned for the disaster in the first place.

GOVERNANCE
Developing Governance Framework on DRRM

The Commission on Audit conducted a disaster risk reduction conference on July 30, 2013, at the COA Professional Development Center, bringing together auditors, local government representatives, and disaster-management specialists to discuss how a stronger governance framework could improve accountability over calamity funds.

Discussions centered on clarifying the roles of national agencies versus local disaster councils, standardizing reporting templates across regions, and building a shared understanding of audit priorities before the next major calamity strikes, rather than only reacting after one has already occurred.

POLICY
Why do we need an Accounting Guide?

Republic Act No. 10121, more popularly known as the Philippine Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Act of 2010, reshaped how national and local funds for calamity response are appropriated, released, and reported. Without a common accounting guide, agencies and LGUs risk recording similar transactions in inconsistent ways, making it harder for auditors to compare fund usage across regions.

A shared accounting guide gives auditors and local finance officers the same reference point for classifying calamity-fund transactions, which in turn makes it easier to trace a peso from the moment it is appropriated to the moment it becomes a delivered relief pack, a rebuilt bridge, or a functioning early-warning sensor.

GOVERNANCE
Field Perspectives on DRRM Governance

Drawing on the same 2013 conference series held at the COA Professional Development Center, this companion piece gathers on-the-ground perspectives from field auditors who were already working directly with local disaster councils at the time the governance framework was being discussed.

Their observations point to a recurring theme: policies that look sound on paper can still break down at the barangay level if local officials are not given clear, practical guidance on how to document relief distribution and infrastructure spending as it happens, rather than reconstructing records after the fact.

PROCUREMENT
Auditing Emergency Procurement Rules

During a declared state of calamity, agencies are allowed to use negotiated and emergency procurement procedures so that relief goods and repair materials can reach affected communities faster than the standard bidding process would normally allow.

That speed comes with a trade-off auditors watch closely: negotiated purchases still need to be reasonably priced and properly documented. This piece walks through how field auditors compare emergency purchase prices against prevailing market rates and canvass sheets from unaffected areas to flag possible overpricing.

✕ Close


FINANCE
Tracing Donor Fund Reconciliation

Large-scale disasters often bring pledges of assistance from foreign governments, multilateral agencies, and private donors, each with its own reporting requirements and timelines. Matching these pledges to what is actually received and spent is one of the more painstaking parts of a post-disaster audit.

This article looks at how auditors reconcile donor commitments with actual bank transfers, in-kind deliveries, and program reports, and why gaps between a pledge announced in the news and the assistance that is formally recorded do not always mean funds went missing, but do need to be explained.

✕ Close


CPA
Inside the Citizen Participatory Audit

The Citizen Participatory Audit program pairs trained COA auditors with community volunteers so that ordinary residents can take part directly in checking disaster-related projects in their own neighborhoods, from small flood-control structures to relief-goods warehouses.

Because community volunteers are the ones who actually live near a project site, they are often the first to notice things a visiting auditor might miss, such as a drainage canal that was never actually completed, or a relief distribution list that does not match the households they know to be in the area.

TACLOBAN
Lessons From Typhoon Yolanda Recovery

More than a decade after Typhoon Yolanda made landfall, auditors continue to draw on the recovery period as a case study in what worked and what did not, particularly around resettlement housing, livelihood assistance, and the reconstruction of public infrastructure in Eastern Visayas.

This retrospective piece revisits those lessons and connects them to current fund-control practices, arguing that the strongest safeguard against future mismanagement is a documentation trail that is built while the recovery is happening, not reconstructed years later from memory.

What is the "Disaster Audit" site by the Commission on Audit, and what should I know about its purpose? ▾

FAQs
More frequently asked questions

What kinds of disaster funds does this site cover? ▾

The portal covers national and local calamity funds, the Quick Response Fund (QRF), the Local Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Fund (LDRRMF), donor and international aid, and other appropriations released for disaster mitigation, response, and rehabilitation.


How often are the audit reports and figures updated? ▾

Reports are published as audit engagements are completed. The live allocation monitor on the homepage is a simulation for illustration and does not reflect real-time disbursement data.


Can I download the full text of an audit report? ▾

Yes. Each report card includes a "Read more" link that opens a summary, with a "Download Full Report" button inside for the complete document.


How do I become a Citizen Participatory Audit (CPA) volunteer? ▾

Volunteers are onboarded through the CPA program together with partner civil society organizations. Check the Citizen Engagement links in the footer or contact the COA Public Information Office to ask about current CPA intakes.


What should I do if I suspect misuse of disaster funds? ▾

Use the "Report Corruption / Fraud" link in the footer under Citizen Engagement. Provide as much detail as you can, such as the agency, project, location, and dates involved, so auditors can look into it.


Are local government units (LGUs) also audited, or only national agencies? ▾

Both. Audits trace funds from national agencies such as DSWD, NDRRMC, and DILG down through provincial, city, municipal, and barangay-level disbursements, since that is where relief goods and cash aid are ultimately distributed.


Does the site accept feedback on report accuracy or broken links? ▾

Yes. Reach the COA Public Information Office through the Contact & Help links in the footer, or the Contact Support link in the top navigation, to flag any errors or issues you find.


Is this site an official Commission on Audit (COA) publication? ▾

Yes, it is presented as an official COA portal dedicated to disaster fund transparency, distinct from the Commission's main corporate website.
